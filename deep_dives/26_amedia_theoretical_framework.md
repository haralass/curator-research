## 1. THE AMEDIA MODEL: FULL ACCOUNT

### 1.1 What Is AMEDIA?

The **AMEDIA Model** (Autobiographical Memory in the Digital Age) is a theoretical framework proposed by Hutmacher, Appel, and Schwan (2024) to systematize the growing literature on how creating, sharing, and accumulating digital data shapes the way individuals remember their personal pasts. It is published as a target article in *Psychological Inquiry* (Vol. 35, No. 2), accompanied by ten peer commentaries and an author response ("Remembering Our Lives in the 21st Century").

The central premise: **autobiographical remembering in the digital age is an iterative process that involves the combination of information stored in one's mind and information stored in external (digital) resources.** This is not a uniform process — it is fundamentally shaped by the technology and data being used.

The model does two things simultaneously: (1) it integrates evidence from cognitive psychology, HCI, and media psychology into a single organizing framework; (2) it generates propositions and research questions to guide empirical work.

### 1.2 Two Core Assumptions

1. **Iterative combination:** Autobiographical remembering is the result of iterating between internal memory traces and externally stored information — not a purely internal reconstruction process. People switch back and forth between what they remember internally and what they find in their digital records.

2. **Prominence of digital external resources:** In the contemporary context, digital external resources play a particularly prominent (and growing) role in this combination process.

Both assumptions are empirically confirmed in Hutmacher, Conrad, Appel, & Schwan (2025) — the birthday memory think-aloud study — where participants (N=40) almost exclusively used digital external resources when remembering events from a year ago, switching iteratively between internal memory and digital records.

### 1.3 Structural Components of AMEDIA

The model specifies two structural components:

**A. Internal Autobiographical Memory**
- Traditional cognitive system: episodic memory (specific events), semantic personal knowledge (general facts about self), and autobiographical knowledge base
- Subject to forgetting, reconstruction, and distortion over time
- Encodes in a lossy, schema-driven, emotion-influenced manner

**B. Externally Recorded Information (External Digital Resources)**
- Information that has been captured and stored in digital form outside the brain
- Critically, the model uses the term **"curated"** rather than "stored" — external records are selective, not necessarily veridical, and reflect choices about what was worth preserving

The relationship between A and B is bidirectional: external records can prime or correct internal memories; internal memories shape what external records one consults and how one interprets them.

### 1.4 Process Components of AMEDIA

Three process components govern how structural components interact:

**1. Encoding**
How an experience is initially registered — both internally (what the person remembers) and externally (what they capture: photos, notes, messages). Recording tools can *divide attention* during the original event, affecting how well it is internally encoded (the photo-taking impairment effect: Henkel, 2014). The act of externally encoding an event may reduce depth of internal encoding.

**2. Curating**
How external records are maintained, organized, selected, and shaped over time. This is the AMEDIA model's distinctive terminological choice: the authors deliberately prefer "curation" over "storage" to capture the active, selective, value-laden nature of what people do with their digital records. Curation involves:
- What is kept vs. deleted
- How records are organized (folders, albums, tags)
- What is shared (social media, messaging) vs. kept private
- How records are edited or annotated after the fact

**3. Retrieving**
How information is recovered — from internal memory, from external resources, or (most typically) from an iterative combination of both. Retrieval from external resources depends on curation quality: how well records were organized and labeled at time of capture affects how findable they are later.

### 1.5 Types of Digital Artifacts AMEDIA Considers

The model explicitly lists and discusses:

- **Photographs and videos** (most extensively studied; photo-taking impairment; lifelogging cameras)
- **Instant messages and text conversations** (WhatsApp, SMS — both the content and metadata)
- **Diary and journaling apps** (e.g., Day One — studied empirically by Hutmacher, 2023)
- **Social media posts** (Facebook memories, Instagram archives)
- **Email archives**
- **Fitness and health tracking data** (quantified self)
- **Location data / GPS traces**
- **Calendar entries**
- **Music listening history** (Spotify, Last.fm)

The model does NOT explicitly discuss generic file system documents (PDFs, Word files, code files, project files) as memory artifacts. This is a significant gap: AMEDIA's digital artifact scope is primarily experiential/social media artifacts, not work or knowledge artifacts.

### 1.6 Does AMEDIA Address File Organization?

**No, not directly.** The model discusses curation as a conceptual dimension but does not operationalize file organization, folder hierarchies, naming conventions, or the cognitive processes involved in managing a file system. This is a notable gap that Curator can fill.

The curation dimension is the closest point of contact: AMEDIA notes that how external records are organized affects how retrievable they are, but it does not theorize the specific mechanisms (semantic labeling, temporal indexing, associative linking) that make files findable.

### 1.7 How AMEDIA Classifies Relationships Between Digital Objects and Memory

AMEDIA frames the relationship through a **functional complementarity** lens: digital records and internal memory are neither redundant nor replacements for each other — they are functionally complementary, each filling gaps the other leaves. The relationship is:

- **Additive:** Digital records can preserve episodic detail that internal memory loses (e.g., exact date, who was present)
- **Corrective:** Digital records can correct distorted or false memories
- **Generative:** Consulting digital records can trigger recall of internal memories not directly captured externally (a photo of a place triggers memories of the whole visit)
- **Potentially distorting:** Digital records can overwrite or reshape internal memories (source monitoring errors; retrospective contamination)
- **Dependent on quality of curation:** A well-curated external record enhances retrieval; a poorly curated or disorganized one may be worse than no record at all

### 1.8 Does AMEDIA Address Forgetting of Digital Objects?

**Partially.** The model acknowledges several dimensions of digital forgetting:

- **Technological obsolescence:** Digital storage formats change; files may become inaccessible (the model notes that analog media like paper diaries outlast many digital formats)
- **Intentional deletion:** People choose to delete records, which is the digital equivalent of motivated forgetting
- **Functional forgetting of digital records:** People accumulate so much digital data that they forget what they have — records exist but are not consulted because the person does not remember they exist or cannot find them
- **Curation as forgetting management:** Hutmacher (2023) found that users of journaling apps feared data loss because "losing the stored data would mean losing one's past"

The model draws on Mayer-Schönberger's (2009) "Delete" framework: digital technology disrupts the natural human balance of remembering and forgetting by defaulting to permanent retention. The AMEDIA model treats intentional forgetting of digital records as an underexplored area requiring more research.

### 1.9 Key Constructs and Dimensions Summary

| Dimension | AMEDIA Construct | Research Questions Generated |
|-----------|-----------------|------------------------------|
| Structural | Internal AM vs. External Digital Records | How do they complement/conflict? |
| Process | Encoding | Does recording reduce internal encoding? |
| Process | Curation | What curation strategies optimize later retrieval? |
| Process | Retrieval | What patterns of internal/external iteration occur? |
| Artifact type | Photo/video vs. text vs. behavioral data | Do different artifact types have different AM functions? |
| Temporal | Density of records over time | Does increased record density change memory quality? |
| Valence | Positive vs. negative events | Are digital records more or less consulted for negative events? |
| Forgetting | Digital permanence vs. natural forgetting | What are effects of inability to forget? |

### 2.1 Empirical Extensions

**Hutmacher, F., Conrad, B., Appel, M., & Schwan, S. (2025).** "Mediated autobiographical remembering in the digital age: Insights from an experimental think-aloud study." *Cognitive Research: Principles and Implications, 10*, Article 18. PMC12045852.
- Preregistered think-aloud study (N=40)
- Task: remember a birthday and a random day from ~1 year ago
- Finding: participants iteratively switched between internal memory and digital external resources; digital resources dominated
- Confirms both core AMEDIA assumptions empirically
- Key detail: participants almost exclusively used digital resources, rarely relied on purely internal memory for events a year old

- Pre-AMEDIA empirical groundwork
- Found users feared data loss as equivalent to losing their past
- Some users worried about being reminded of negative events
- Documents resistance to algorithmic curation ("I want to choose what I remember")
- Directly relevant to Curator's design: users have strong preferences about curation agency

## 3. AUTOBIOGRAPHICAL MEMORY + PERSONAL INFORMATION MANAGEMENT

### 3.1 The PIM Research Stream

Personal Information Management (PIM) is an established HCI research area studying how people acquire, organize, maintain, and retrieve information for everyday use (Jones & Teevan, 2007, *Personal Information Management*, UW Press). It has historically been treated as separate from autobiographical memory research — a significant interdisciplinary gap.

Key PIM findings relevant to Curator:

**Jones, W. (2007).** *Keeping Found Things Found: The Study and Practice of Personal Information Management.* Morgan Kaufmann.
- Documents the "PIM fragmentation problem": people's information is scattered across tools (email, files, notes, bookmarks) without integration
- Distinguishes between *finding* (known-item search) and *re-finding* (re-accessing something encountered before) — re-finding is dominant and much harder
- Files accumulate faster than organizational schemes can accommodate

**"Towards Memory Supporting Personal Information Management Tools"** (Strathclyde). Available at: https://strathprints.strath.ac.uk/2395/6/strathprints002395.pdf
- Explicitly argues that PIM tools should be redesigned around how human memory works, not how filing systems work
- Reviews failure modes of folder hierarchies from a memory psychology perspective

**"Supporting Human Memory with a Personal Digital Lifetime Store"** (Microsoft Research, Bell et al., 2007). In Jones & Teevan (eds.).
- MyLifeBits project: total capture of personal digital life
- Found that raw volume of capture creates retrieval problems rather than solving them
- Conclusion: organization/curation infrastructure is more important than capture capacity

**"Informing Augmented Memory System Design Through Autobiographical Memory Theory"** (Academia.edu).
- Directly bridges AM theory and system design
- Proposes AM constructs (temporal context, emotional salience, self-relevance) as design primitives for augmented memory systems

### 3.2 Curator's Position in This Gap

Curator occupies the intersection of AMEDIA and PIM: it is a system that treats personal files as autobiographical artifacts (AMEDIA framing) and applies intelligent organization to reduce curation burden (PIM framing). No existing system combines these two framings computationally.

## 4. EXTENDED MIND THESIS APPLIED TO PERSONAL FILES

### 4.1 Clark & Chalmers (1998): The Core Argument

Clark, A., & Chalmers, D. (1998). "The Extended Mind." *Analysis, 58*(1), 7–19.

The **Extended Mind Thesis** (EMT) argues that cognitive processes need not be confined to the brain — they extend into the world when external resources function in the same role as internal cognitive states. The **parity principle**: if an external process does what an internal process would do (if it were internal), it deserves equal cognitive status.

The classic example: **Otto and Inga.**
- Inga remembers where the museum is (internal memory)
- Otto writes everything in a notebook because he has Alzheimer's; consults it to find the museum
- Both have equivalent *dispositional beliefs* about the museum's location — one stored internally, one externally
- Conclusion: Otto's notebook is part of his cognitive system

**Criteria for cognitive extension (coupling-constitution conditions):**
1. The resource must be reliably available and accessible
2. The information retrieved must be directly and automatically endorsed (no scrutiny typically applied to retrieved results)
3. The information must have been consciously endorsed at some point in the past

**Application to personal file systems:**
A well-organized personal file system (e.g., a Curator-managed file system) satisfies all three criteria:
- Files are reliably accessible (local/cloud storage)
- Curated files are directly trusted by the user
- Files are products of conscious past encoding decisions

Under EMT, **Curator-managed files are literally part of the user's extended cognitive system.** Curator is not an external tool helping the user — it is co-constitutive of the user's mind.

### 4.2 Digital Extensions and HCI

**"Cognitive Offloading and the Extended Digital Self"** — chapter in Springer (available via Illinois Experts and ResearchGate).
- Bridges EMT to HCI and ubiquitous computing
- Argues smartphones and personal digital environments constitute an "extended digital self"
- The proliferation of immersive, pervasive, and ambient computing raises new questions about the shifting interplay between internal and external memory

The EMT framing is increasingly influential in HCI contexts where personal devices are treated not as mere tools but as components of extended cognitive architecture. Curator's theoretical positioning benefits from EMT: the system is not a productivity tool but a cognitive prosthetic — a literal extension of the user's autobiographical memory system.

## 5. COGNITIVE OFFLOADING

### 5.1 Risko & Gilbert (2016): The Defining Framework

Risko, E. F., & Gilbert, S. J. (2016). "Cognitive Offloading." *Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 20*(9), 676–688. [PDF: samgilbert.net/pubs/Risko2016TiCS.pdf]

**Definition:** Cognitive offloading is "the use of physical action to alter the information processing requirements of a task to reduce cognitive demand."

**Key theoretical contributions:**
- Offloading is a *strategic* behavior: people choose when to offload based on metacognitive assessment of memory capacity vs. external resource reliability
- Offloading changes the *nature* of cognitive processing, not just the load — it creates new task structures
- Long-term consequences: offloading can reduce internal encoding (use-it-or-lose-it), but this may be adaptive if external resources are reliable
- Introduces **metacognitive model of offloading**: decisions to offload depend on beliefs about internal memory capabilities and environmental resource quality

**Types of offloading:**
1. **Motor offloading:** using gesture/action to alter task (e.g., rearranging tiles)
2. **Storage offloading:** writing things down, saving files, taking photos
3. **Prospective offloading (intention offloading):** setting reminders, creating to-do lists, strategically placing objects

### 5.2 Intention Offloading and Prospective Memory

**Outsourcing Memory to External Tools: A Review of 'Intention Offloading'** (Psychonomic Bulletin & Review, 2022). PMC9971128.
- Reviews the literature on using external tools to remember to do future things
- Key finding: people set reminders not just for high-importance tasks but to free cognitive resources — even low-stakes tasks get offloaded when the cost of offloading is low
- **Reminder-type matters:** physical reminders (objects placed strategically) vs. digital alerts vs. calendar entries have different effectiveness profiles

**Benefits from Prospective Memory Offloading Depend on Memory Load** (Peper et al., 2022, JEPLMC).
- Benefits of offloading are greatest under high memory load — exactly the condition Curator users face (large, complex file systems)

**Cost Avoidance Underlies Decisions to Use Prospective Memory Reminders** (Ball & Peper, 2025, Memory & Cognition).
- People avoid setting reminders when the effort to set them is high — supports Curator's design principle of low-friction automatic organization

**Reminders Eliminate Age-Related Declines in Prospective Memory** (PMC11781985, 2025).
- External reminders (digital cues, file annotations) fully compensate for age-related prospective memory decline
- Curator's semantic annotations function as prospective memory cues embedded in the file system

### 5.3 Cognitive Offloading to Files: Direct Relevance to Curator

When users save a file, name it, or place it in a folder, they are performing **storage offloading** — externalizing information they expect to need later. Curator intervenes in this process by:
1. Reducing the cognitive cost of curation (automatic organization)
2. Improving the reliability of the external resource (semantic understanding, intelligent retrieval)
3. Enabling **prospective offloading through file biography**: files carry their history of use as contextual cues for future retrieval

The offloading literature predicts that reducing curation cost (Curator's intervention) will increase offloading behavior — users will save more and organize less effortfully, trusting the system to maintain findability.

## 6. TRANSACTIVE MEMORY AND PERSONAL FILE SYSTEMS

### 6.1 Wegner's Transactive Memory System (1987)

Wegner, D. M. (1987). "Transactive Memory: A Contemporary Analysis of the Group Mind." In B. Mullen & G. R. Goethals (Eds.), *Theories of Group Behavior*. Springer.

Transactive memory was originally a theory of *group* cognition — how couples, teams, and organizations distribute knowledge across members so that no individual needs to know everything.

**Core insight:** People encode not just *information* but *meta-information* about where to find information (who knows what, where it is stored). This creates a distributed memory system that exceeds individual capacity.

**Key components:**
- **Encoding:** individuals store information in domains they are responsible for
- **Storage:** distributed across members (each person is an external memory for others)
- **Retrieval:** via directory (meta-knowledge about who/what stores what)

### 6.2 Transactive Memory in the Digital Age

**Transactive Memory Systems in the 'Digital Age'** (Springer, 2018). Chapter available: https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-658-22180-5_4
- Argues that digital tools (search engines, knowledge bases, cloud storage) now function as transactive partners in the same way human members of a transactive system do
- The Google/internet now functions as a transactive memory system for factual knowledge (Sparrow, Liu, & Wegner, 2011)

**Sparrow, B., Liu, J., & Wegner, D. M. (2011).** "Google Effects on Memory: Cognitive Consequences of Having Information at Our Fingertips." *Science, 333*, 776–778.
- When people expect to be able to Google information later, they encode it less deeply internally
- They do, however, encode *where* to find information (source memory) — consistent with transactive memory
- The internet has become an external transactive partner, replacing some inter-personal transactive memory

**Application to personal file systems:**
A personal file system is a *single-agent transactive memory system* — the user outsources knowledge storage to the file system and maintains a mental directory (meta-memory) of where things are. When this directory degrades (the user forgets what files exist, or where they are), the transactive relationship breaks down: the information is stored but unretrievable.

**Curator's intervention:** Curator maintains and repairs the transactive directory. It generates semantic metadata, surface forgotten files, and reconstructs associative paths between files — functioning as a cognitive prosthetic for failing meta-memory about file locations.

## 7. PROSPECTIVE MEMORY AND PERSONAL COMPUTING

### 7.1 The Prospective Memory Problem in File Management

Prospective memory is the ability to remember to perform an action at a future time or event. In the file management context, prospective memory failures take forms like:
- "I saved a relevant document somewhere but forgot to return to it"
- "I meant to continue this project but the file was out of sight/out of mind"
- "I remember I have notes on this topic but cannot recall where"

Research establishes:
- Prospective memory is highly susceptible to interference and forgetting when cues are absent
- Contextual cues at intended-action time dramatically improve prospective memory
- Digital reminders (alerts, to-do items) reliably compensate for prospective memory failures

### 7.2 Files as Prospective Memory Artifacts

Files are often created *prospectively* — as reminders to future self (draft documents, saved links, work-in-progress folders). This is a prospective memory function that AMEDIA does not theorize but that Curator must handle. File biography (tracking when files were created vs. last accessed vs. modified) captures prospective memory patterns: a file created but never accessed again likely failed as a prospective memory cue.

**Influence of Physical Effort of Reminder-Setting on Strategic Offloading** (PMC11103908, 2024).
- Even small friction in creating reminders reduces offloading
- Curator's zero-friction automatic organization lowers the cost of prospective offloading to files

## 8. DIGITAL FORGETTING AND PERSONAL ARCHIVES

### 8.1 Mayer-Schönberger (2009): Delete

Mayer-Schönberger, V. (2009). *Delete: The Virtue of Forgetting in the Digital Age.* Princeton University Press.

- Digital technology defaults to permanent retention, inverting the human cognitive default of forgetting
- Proposes **expiration dates** on electronic files as a technical solution to digital permanence
- Argues that inability to forget distorts personal identity, social relationships, and institutional memory
- The "right to be forgotten" emerges from this framework

**Curator relevance:** Curator must have a principled stance on digital forgetting. Options:
- Surface "forgotten" files to restore access (anti-forgetting)
- Enable intentional archival/deletion of files user no longer wants foregrounded (managed forgetting)
- Respect user preferences about which memories to foreground vs. recede

### 8.2 Harvard LIL Analysis (2025)

"Live and Let Die: Rethinking Personal Digital Archiving, Memory, and Forgetting Through a Library Lens" (Harvard Library Innovation Lab, 2025). https://lil.law.harvard.edu/blog/2025/08/20/live-and-let-die-rethinking-personal-digital-archiving-memory-and-forgetting-through-a-library-lens/
- Digital storage abundance creates paradox: unlimited space but poor retrieval
- "Search functions help us find known items, but struggle with the forgotten"
- Advocates for active curation (selective preservation) over passive accumulation
- Frames forgetting as adaptive: not all digital records should be preserved indefinitely

### 8.3 AMEDIA on Forgetting of Digital Records

The AMEDIA authors identify three types of digital forgetting that occur despite technical persistence:
1. **Technological obsolescence forgetting:** records become inaccessible due to format/platform changes
2. **Motivational deletion:** users intentionally delete records they no longer want
3. **Functional forgetting:** records exist but are never consulted because user does not remember they exist

Type 3 is the most relevant for Curator: **functional forgetting of digital records** is a retrieval problem, not a storage problem. Curator addresses this by making functionally forgotten files discoverable through semantic search and proactive surfacing.

### 9.1 FileGram (2026)

Liu, S. et al. (2026). "FileGram: Grounding Agent Personalization in File-System Behavioral Traces." arXiv:2604.04901.
- AI agent integrated into local file system
- Builds long-term behavioral memory from cross-session file activity
- Three components: FileGramEngine (data generation), FileGramBench (evaluation), FileGramOS (memory architecture with procedural/semantic/episodic channels)
- Closest existing system to Curator in technical approach

**Differences from Curator:**
- FileGram focuses on *agent personalization* (making AI coworker smarter) not on *user memory augmentation*
- FileGram does not use AMEDIA or autobiographical memory framing
- FileGram does not address curation decisions, file biography, or the psychology of digital forgetting
- Curator's theoretical contribution is the AMEDIA/cognitive psychology grounding that FileGram lacks

### 9.2 MyLifeBits (Bell et al., Microsoft Research, 2007)

- Total lifelogging project: captured everything Gordon Bell did/said/read
- Found retrieval from massive unorganized archives was poor
- Demonstrated that curation infrastructure (not capture capacity) is the bottleneck

### 10.2 AMEDIA's Gap: Work/Knowledge Files

AMEDIA focuses on experiential artifacts (photos, messages, diaries). Curator extends the AMEDIA framework to **work and knowledge artifacts** — the documents, data files, code, and notes that constitute a person's intellectual and professional life. These are:
- Not primarily aimed at memory preservation (unlike diaries/photos)
- Yet serve autobiographical-memory-equivalent functions: they encode past decisions, preserve project context, carry personal history of intellectual development
- Subject to the same encoding, curation, retrieval dynamics that AMEDIA identifies

This extension of AMEDIA from experiential to knowledge artifacts is Curator's primary theoretical contribution to the cognitive science framing.

### 10.3 Cognitive Offloading: Curator's Design Rationale

Risko & Gilbert's (2016) metacognitive model predicts:
- Users offload more when internal memory feels unreliable
- Users offload more when external resources feel reliable
- Users offload less when offloading is effortful

Curator's design directly addresses all three levers:
- Makes internal limitations salient (surfaces forgotten files, shows access gaps)
- Makes the file system more reliable (semantic organization, intelligent retrieval)
- Reduces offloading cost (automatic organization, zero-friction saving)

Expected behavioral outcome (testable): Curator users will save and organize more files, retrieve more successfully, and report lower cognitive load in file management tasks.

### 10.4 Extended Mind: Curator as Cognitive Constitution

Under Clark & Chalmers (1998):
- A standard file system fails the EMT coupling conditions: it is not reliably organized, retrieval requires scrutiny, and meta-memory about file locations degrades
- Curator-managed files satisfy the EMT coupling conditions: semantic organization is reliable, search returns trusted results, and file biography makes past decisions transparent
- Therefore, a Curator-managed file system more fully constitutes an extended cognitive system than a standard file system

This is a theoretically significant claim: Curator doesn't just help users manage files — it completes the cognitive loop that makes files genuine extensions of autobiographical memory.

### 10.5 Transactive Memory: Curator as Directory Maintenance

Under Wegner's (1987) transactive memory framework:
- Users maintain a mental "directory" of what is in their file system (meta-memory about file locations)
- This directory degrades as the file system grows and ages — files are forgotten even though they exist
- Curator functions as an external directory maintenance system: it rebuilds and repairs the user's meta-memory about their files through semantic indexing and proactive surfacing
- Curator effectively extends the individual transactive memory system from brain+filing-heuristics to brain+Curator-maintained-semantic-index

## 11. FRAMING CURATOR'S CONTRIBUTION FOR IUI 2027

### 11.1 The Core Theoretical Claim

Curator's IUI 2027 paper should frame its contribution as:

> **The first system to computationally operationalize the AMEDIA model's curation process for knowledge-work file systems, extending autobiographical memory theory from experiential artifacts (photos, messages) to intellectual artifacts (documents, data, code), while grounding its design in cognitive offloading theory and extended mind thesis.**

### 11.2 Key Novelty Arguments

**vs. FileGram (2026):** FileGram personalizes an AI agent using file traces; Curator augments user autobiographical memory using cognitive psychology theory. Different goal, different framing, different evaluation criteria.

**vs. PIM literature (Jones, 2007):** PIM addresses findability from an information science perspective; Curator addresses it from a cognitive science perspective, generating different design principles (e.g., curation quality as cognitive offloading cost, file biography as prospective memory support).

**vs. Lifelogging (2015–2022):** Lifelogging captures experience passively; Curator works with existing file creation behavior (no new capture device), applying intelligence to already-existing digital records.

**vs. AMEDIA theory itself:** AMEDIA identifies curation as theoretically critical but does not operationalize it computationally. Curator is the first computational realization of AMEDIA's curation dimension.

### 11.3 Evaluation Implications from Theory

The AMEDIA/offloading/EMT framing generates specific hypotheses to test at IUI 2027:

1. **Retrieval quality hypothesis** (AMEDIA): Curator improves autobiographical retrieval from file system (users can find more of their files, more quickly, with higher confidence)
2. **Offloading behavior hypothesis** (Risko & Gilbert): Curator increases frequency of file saving/organization behavior (users offload more when the system is reliable)
3. **Cognitive coupling hypothesis** (Clark & Chalmers): Curator-managed files show higher EMT coupling scores (reliability, trust, automaticity of retrieval)
4. **Functional forgetting hypothesis** (AMEDIA/Mayer-Schönberger): Curator reduces functional forgetting — users rediscover files they had functionally forgotten
5. **Prospective memory hypothesis**: Files created with Curator integration are more likely to be retrieved when relevant (prospective memory cue effectiveness)

### 11.4 Paper Structure Suggestion

1. Introduction: the problem of digital personal archives as functionally forgotten memories
2. Theoretical Background: AMEDIA model → curation gap → extended mind → cognitive offloading → transactive memory
3. Curator System: design principles derived from each theoretical construct
4. Evaluation: mixed methods (file retrieval tasks + subjective memory measures + behavioral logging)
5. Discussion: Curator as operationalization of AMEDIA curation; extensions to knowledge artifacts; design implications for PIM tools
